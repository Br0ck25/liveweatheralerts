Hazard Library

Each card explains the threat in plain language and gives immediate actions.

Thunderstorm

Severe storms can produce large hail, damaging wind, lightning, and flash flooding.

  • Move indoors when storms approach.
  • Avoid windows during severe warnings.
  • Do not drive through flooded roads.
Read official guidance

Tornado

Tornadoes can form quickly and are not always easy to see.

  • Know your safe place now.
  • Treat tornado warnings as immediate action.
  • Stay away from windows and exterior walls.
Read official guidance

Flood

Flooding can happen fast and often impacts roads before people expect it.

  • Never drive into flooded water.
  • Move to higher ground when flood warnings are issued.
  • Avoid low crossings and creek roads.
Read official guidance

Lightning

Lightning can strike before heavy rain reaches your location.

  • When thunder roars, go indoors.
  • Avoid open fields, trees, and water.
  • Wait at least 30 minutes after last thunder.
Read official guidance

Heat

Heat illness can develop quickly, especially for older adults, children, and outdoor workers.

  • Hydrate often and rest in shade or AC.
  • Limit activity during hottest hours.
  • Check on neighbors and family members.
Read official guidance

Winter Weather

Snow, ice, and wind chill can create dangerous travel and exposure conditions.

  • Slow down and avoid unnecessary travel.
  • Carry warm layers and emergency supplies.
  • Watch for changing road conditions.
Read official guidance

Wildfire

Dry fuels, wind, and low humidity can spread fire rapidly.

  • Follow local burn restrictions.
  • Prepare an evacuation go-bag.
  • Leave early if evacuation orders are issued.
Read official guidance

Rip Current

Rip currents can pull swimmers away from shore quickly.

  • Swim near lifeguards when possible.
  • If caught, swim parallel to shore.
  • Float and signal for help if tired.
Read official guidance

Hurricane

Hurricanes bring wind, surge, inland flooding, and long-duration impacts.

  • Know your evacuation zone.
  • Stock supplies before conditions worsen.
  • Do not focus only on the center track.
Read official guidance

Weather FAQ Quick Answers

Watch vs Warning

A watch means severe weather is possible. A warning means severe weather is happening or expected very soon.

What counts as severe thunderstorm weather?

In simple terms: large hail, very strong wind, or tornado potential from thunderstorms.

Why percentages can look small

Even moderate-looking percentages can signal meaningful risk compared to a normal day at one location.

Risk levels

Marginal to High means increasing organized severe storm threat. Higher levels need more planning and faster response.

Tornado Basics In Plain Language

Core truth

Tornadoes can form quickly and are not always visibly obvious. Use warnings and alerts, not visuals alone.

Best action

When a tornado warning is issued, move immediately to your safe place and stay there until danger passes.

Common myth

“I can wait to see it.” This delays action and increases risk. Early movement is safer than late movement.
